Here are the most common categories of grammar mistakes found in professional writing, emails, reports, blogs, and user-generated content.

Category	Example Mistake	Correct Form
Subject-Verb Agreement	“The list of items are ready.”	“The list of items is ready.”
Verb Tense Consistency	“She started the project and finishes it yesterday.”	“She started the project and finished it yesterday.”
Sentence Fragments	“Because the meeting was canceled.”	“The meeting was canceled.”
Run-On Sentences	“I finished the report I sent it to the team.”	“I finished the report, and I sent it to the team.”
Comma Splices	“The app crashed, we restarted it.”	“The app crashed, so we restarted it.”
Misplaced Modifiers	“Walking to work, the rain soaked my clothes.”	“While I was walking to work, the rain soaked my clothes.”
Dangling Modifiers	“After reviewing the code, the bug was obvious.”	“After reviewing the code, I found the bug was obvious.”
Pronoun Agreement	“Every developer should test their code.”*	Style-dependent
Pronoun Reference	“John told Mike he was wrong.”	Clarify who “he” refers to.
Incorrect Pronoun Case	“Me and Sarah went.”	“Sarah and I went.”
Apostrophe Errors	“The servers performance”	“The server’s performance”
Its vs. It’s	“Its a great product.”	“It’s a great product.”
Their / There / They’re	“Their going over there.”	“They’re going over there.”
Your / You’re	“Your welcome.”	“You’re welcome.”
Affect / Effect	“The change effected performance.”	Depends on intended meaning.
Who / Whom	“Who did you send it to?” (often accepted)	“Whom did you send it to?”
Parallel Structure	“The job requires coding, testing, and to document.”	“The job requires coding, testing, and documenting.”
Incorrect Comparisons	“This laptop is faster than any laptop in the office.”	“This laptop is faster than any other laptop in the office.”
Double Negatives	“I don’t need no help.”	“I don’t need any help.”
Article Errors	“She is engineer.”	“She is an engineer.”
Preposition Errors	“Different than” vs. “Different from”	Depends on style guide.
Capitalization Errors	“the United States”	“The United States” when starting a sentence.
Punctuation Errors	Missing commas, periods, quotation marks	Correct punctuation.

4. Run-on sentences

6. Apostrophe misuse
7. Pronoun ambiguity
8. Misplaced modifiers

10. Parallel structure errors

Common Errors in AI-Generated Text

Modern grammar checkers frequently detect:

* Repetitive sentence structure
* Excessive passive voice
* Wordiness
* Unclear antecedents (“it”, “this”, “they”)
* Overuse of transition words (“however”, “moreover”, “furthermore”)
* Inconsistent terminology
* Redundant phrases (“future plans”, “end result”, “completely finished”)

Common Errors in Student Writing

* Comma splices
* Run-on sentences
* Sentence fragments
* Verb tense shifts
* Weak thesis support
* Informal language in formal writing
* Incorrect citation formatting

If you’re building a grammar checker (for example, in Neutrino Docs), I would group errors into these higher-level categories:

1. Grammar
2. Punctuation
3. Spelling
4. Style
5. Clarity
6. Conciseness
7. Consistency
8. Readability

This taxonomy closely matches the approach used by tools such as Grammarly, Microsoft Editor, and LanguageTool.
